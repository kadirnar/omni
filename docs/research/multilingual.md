# Multi-language support for a Mimi-token 12.5 Hz speech LLM

## (a) Mimi multilinguality
Mimi (CC-BY license, 12.5 Hz, 1.1 kbps) was trained by Kyutai on predominantly-English data with semantic codebook 0 distilled from WavLM (English SSL). Direct evidence of non-English use: Kyutai's own TTS 1.6B (Jul 2025) and Unmute run En/Fr on Mimi; Pocket TTS (Jan 2026, Mimi-based) added De/Es/Pt/It by May 2026 — so Kyutai itself ships 6 European languages through frozen Mimi. Strongest third-party datapoint: **J-Moshi** (arXiv 2506.02979, Jun 2025) froze Mimi for Japanese after finding it "has a certain level of ability to encode and resynthesize Japanese speech without additional training"; resynthesis naturalness was 3.9/5.0 vs 4.46 ground truth, and authors state "Mimi will also need to be adapted to Japanese in the future." For tonal languages the risk is concrete: the DualCodec paper (2025) reports Mimi (and SpeechTokenizer) produce "excessively large Chinese WERs" in AR+SoundStorm TTS, attributed to the semantic RVQ-1 lacking pitch/tone information — exactly our codebook-0. Mitigation: we generate all 32 codebooks autoregressively with text conditioning (a 2026 streaming-codec study finds Mimi-32 gives best intelligibility among its configs), so acoustic codebooks can carry tone, but the semantic codebook's English bias remains a Gate-0 risk for Mandarin. No per-language reconstruction table for Mimi exists anywhere I found. Fallback codecs (architecture-breaking — 25/50 Hz, different grid): XCodec2 (hf-audio/xcodec2; LLaSA family), improved X-Codec2.0 at 25 Hz/24 kHz (arXiv 2601.20185, evaluated on 116-language Common Voice 17), NVIDIA NanoCodec (Aug 2025). Swapping destroys the 12.5 Hz DESIGN_V3 frame grid; treat only as last resort.

## (c) Tokenizer sizing vs the 12.5 tok/s ceiling
Moshi uses a 32k SentencePiece unigram tokenizer "mostly targeting English"; its inner monologue is ~65% PAD at 12.5 Hz ⇒ ~4.4 text tok/s. J-Moshi (Japanese 32k tokenizer) saw 88% PAD ⇒ ~1.5 tok/s. Fertility literature (Tokenizer Choice for LLM Training, arXiv 2310.08754): 33k suffices for English; a 5-language multilingual tokenizer needs up to ~3× that; fertility drops monotonically with vocab size. Big speech LLMs use huge text vocabs (Qwen2.5-Omni 151,643; GLM-4-Voice = GLM-4 vocab + 16k speech tokens, interleaves text:speech 13:26). Rate math at conversational speed: En 2.5 w/s × ~1.4 tok/w ≈ 3.5 tok/s; Fr/De/Es ≈ 4–5.5; Turkish ~1.7–2 w/s × ~2.2–2.6 tok/w ≈ 4–5 tok/s (Latin script — byte fallback harmless) — all safe. Mandarin ≈ 268 chars/min ≈ 4.5 chars/s: with CJK chars in-vocab ≈ 3.5–4.5 tok/s (safe); **with UTF-8 byte fallback (3 B/char) ≈ 13.5 tok/s — exceeds 12.5** and breaks text-ahead. Same for kana/hangul. Conclusion: CJK must be in the vocab (≈6–8k chars + merges), pushing us to **48k–64k**; 64k × d=2048 embedding = 131M params (~13% of 1.0B) — 48k is the sweet spot unless Ja/Ko are in scope.

## (d) Language control
Whisper convention: 99 language tokens, sequence `<|startoftranscript|><|xx|><|transcribe|>`; LID = model predicts the language token itself (auto-detect) or it's forced (locked). Qwen2.5-Omni speech output was Zh/En only; Qwen3-Omni expanded to 10 output languages; GLM-4-Voice is Zh/En. Recommended for us: allocate per-language tags in the 53 free special ids (`<lang:en>`…), emit tag at each turn start on the text stream; train with tag forced (locked mode) and predicted (auto mode, Whisper-style) — both behaviors from one convention.

## RECOMMENDATION
V1 languages: **En, Fr, De (tier 1), Zh (tier 1 with codec gate), Tr (tier 2: understand+respond, accept lower voice naturalness), Ja/Ko deferred**. Hours (commercially clean): En ~137k (Emilia-YODAS 92k + MLS 44.5k), Fr ~8.5k, De ~7.6k, Ko 7.3k available if wanted; Zh is license-constrained (~0.3k CC-BY + CommonVoice CC0) — cover assistant-side with VibeVoice/CosyVoice3 synthesis and accept CC-BY-NC Emilia-Zh only if NC is tolerable. Turkish: ~130 h CommonVoice (CC0) + YODAS2-tr + synthetic dialogue via Chatterbox Multilingual (MIT, has Turkish + emotion control) — viable but thin; plan ~500–1,000 h total.

Tokenizer: train **48k byte-BPE** (up from 32k; embedding cost at 1B is acceptable, 64k only if Ja/Ko enter scope), on fineweb-2 (ODC-By) mix ≈ En 35%, Zh 20%, Fr/De/Tr 10% each, Es 5%, code/misc 10%, plus ASR transcripts. Must include ~6–8k CJK characters: with byte fallback Mandarin hits ~13.5 tok/s > 12.5 budget; in-vocab it runs ~3.5–4.5 tok/s. Expected monologue rates: En ~3.5–4.4, Fr/De ~4–5.5, Tr ~4–5, Zh ~4–4.5 tok/s — all under 12.5 with margin. Use 6 of the 53 free special ids as Whisper-style `<lang:xx>` turn-start tags; train both forced (locked) and model-predicted (auto-detect).

Gate-0 codec test (1 day, CPU-OK): Mimi encode→decode FLEURS+CV test sets per language at 8/16/32 codebooks; measure ΔWER (Whisper-large-v3 on resynthesis vs original), UTMOSv2, and a Mandarin tone minimal-pair intelligibility probe. Pass: ΔWER <5 pts absolute at 32 codebooks. Mandarin is the expected failure mode (DualCodec evidence); if it fails, drop Zh to tier 2 rather than swapping codecs — every alternative breaks the 12.5 Hz grid.
